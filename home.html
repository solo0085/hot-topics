The Value of Money

It may seem that money is a sure path to prestige and happiness. After all, our highest-paid citizens are held up as role models of success, living seemingly perfect, enviable lives. In recent studies, scientists have found that the relationship between wealth and well-being is unclear. While some studies link wealth to well-being, others show that, after a certain point, a higher income will not bring more happiness or life satisfaction.

University at Buffalo researcher Lora Parks and her colleagues examined what happens when people associate their self-worth with financial success, scoring high on the "Financial Contingency of Self-worth" scale, or FCWS. The researchers found that by doing so, people engaged in more social comparisons, experienced more stress and anxiety, and felt less autonomy, compared to those who rated their income for income, regardless of their actual economic status. Do not bind self-worth.

"People in this society are often looking for money, and they don't think there's anything bad about it," Park says. "But in terms of your psychological well-being, there are all kinds of negative consequences." It can also affect your problem-solving ability. Park and her colleagues randomly assigned participants to write about their dissatisfaction with one aspect of their financial situation – such as not having enough money to pay rent – ​​or their academic performance, such as poor. Getting test grades. Afterwards, they described what coping strategies they would use in response to the situation.

Research assistants analyzed the essays and found that participants who scored higher in the FCSW used more emotionally negative words and reported more disruption strategies – such as skipping or avoiding solutions – than those who scored lower in the FCSW. When writing about a financial strain versus an academic strain in comparison. None of the results were affected by the actual income of the students. People who are facing a problem should focus on ways to solve it logically, says Park. "But what we found is that the high financial contingency of self-worth somehow blocks that response."
